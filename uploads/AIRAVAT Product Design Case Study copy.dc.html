02

Research

Literature Survey

Dihedral wings carry payload and stay stable.

Dihedral wing geometry was identified as the configuration best suited to a high payload fraction while remaining aerodynamically stable. Raising the wingtips improves lateral stability and payload margin, at the cost of higher structural loads at the root chord — the trade-off that shaped our wing engineering.

Why it matters
Why study an existing commercial airframe before designing your own?
Dihedral wings have carried heavy loads safely on production aircraft for decades; borrowing that proven principle avoids re-learning a stability lesson the industry already paid for.
Airfoil Selection

Modifying a proven profile for more lift.

The Wortmann FX 63-137 airfoil was chosen for its lift characteristics, then reshaped further — thickening camber and adjusting the trailing edge — for better values across the board.

Why it matters
Why does a higher Cl max matter?
More lift per unit of wing area means a smaller, lighter wing can carry the same payload, directly cutting drag, material cost, and build time.
Airfoil Selection

Performance holds up across the polar.

Across drag polar, lift curve, and lift-to-drag ratio, the modified profile (blue) tracks above the Wortmann FX 63-137 baseline (magenta) through the working angle-of-attack range — the basis for carrying it into the full wing design.

Why it matters
Why check performance across the whole angle-of-attack range, not just one number?
A real flight climbs, cruises, and banks at different angles; an airfoil that only wins at one angle can quietly underperform, or stall, the moment conditions change.

Wing Configuration

A hybrid rectangular-dihedral wing.

A 3° dihedral angle begins after 60% of the wing, keeping the inner wing rectangular for structural simplicity while the outer section delivers the roll stability a heavy payload demands.

Why it matters
Why does the dihedral angle matter for an uncrewed aircraft?
Dihedral is a passive, mechanical way to keep the aircraft self-righting in roll, critical when there is no pilot on board to correct a wing drop.
Wing Configuration — Iteration

Finding the dihedral break point.

Lift and drag were simulated for dihedral sections starting at seven span positions. At 0.60 m, drag and lift both ease slightly, but roll stability improves — an acceptable trade at this aircraft's low flight speeds.

Why it matters
Why simulate seven different break points instead of picking one by eye?
Guessing a structural break point risks locking in a wing that is either too weak at the root or unnecessarily draggy; testing the range finds the true best trade-off.

Rectangular, semi-monocoque.

Why does the tail design matter as much as the wing?
Every gram and every unit of drag on the tail is subtracted from the payload and flight-time budget of every single flight; a V-tail buys back both.
Landing Gear & Wing Mount

One part, three jobs.

Tricycle gear was preferred for takeoff and landing stability; a tail-dragger adds the angle of attack needed prior to takeoff. A single 3D-printed bulkhead then does triple duty — carrying the wing at top, gear at bottom, and the payload bay at its center.

Why it matters
Why does combining three jobs into one printed part matter?
Fewer parts and joints mean fewer places to fail on a rough landing, and a damaged bulkhead can be reprinted overnight instead of rebuilding the whole airframe.
Propulsive System

Motor and propeller, matched by data.

A battery-powered 380 KV motor turns a 22×10E propeller at the nose. The pairing was read directly off the manufacturer's thrust, RPM, and efficiency curves rather than assumed.

Why it matters
Why match the motor and propeller from data instead of a rule of thumb?
A mismatched pair wastes battery charge as heat and noise instead of thrust, directly shortening the aircraft's already-tight 18-minute flight window.
Innovation

Four decisions that set this airframe apart.

A reshaped airfoil, not a stock one

The Wortmann FX 63-137 was re-profiled for +19% Cl max and a higher Cl/Cd — gains earned through iteration, not catalog selection.

A dihedral break point tuned by data

Seven span positions were simulated to find 0.60 m — the point where roll stability improves at a drag cost the low-speed mission can absorb.

A V-tail doing a T-tail's job for less

Ruddervators combine rudder and elevator into two surfaces, cutting friction drag and tail weight while keeping full maneuverability.

One printed part, three structural roles

A single 3D-printed bulkhead carries the wing, the landing gear, and the payload bay — fewer joints, less weight, simpler assembly.

Why it matters
Why call these four decisions out on their own slide?
These are the choices a buyer or investor will ask to defend; showing they were deliberate trade-offs, not defaults, is what separates a product from a prototype.
04

Test

Modelling

The complete airframe, assembled.

Why it matters
Why model the full assembly before cutting any material?
This is where a wing spar colliding with the payload bay, or a servo fouling the landing gear, gets caught on a screen, instead of on the workbench after the parts are already built.
Structural Analysis

Two structures, stress-tested in Ansys.

Wing structure

Flange and spar wing structures were tested under identical loading in Ansys Mechanical, comparing deflection and stress at maximum wing loading to select the stiffer, lighter option.

Landing gear drop test

A drop test at 14 m/s in SolidWorks evaluated impact deformation across three touch-down angles — 130°, 135°, and 140° — to validate gear survivability.

Balsa wood
Primary structure material
Aluminum 1060
Fittings and hardware
Why it matters
Why simulate structural failure instead of just building and testing it?
A wing or gear failure in flight is expensive and dangerous; running the drop test and load case in software finds the breaking point safely, before it happens for real.
Flow Analysis — Airfoil

A fine mesh around the modified profile.

The modified Wortmann airfoil was meshed with tetrahedral elements at linear order, dense enough at the leading and trailing edges to resolve flow separation in the pressure and velocity solves that follow.

Why it matters
Why does mesh quality matter before running any flow simulation?
A coarse mesh can smooth over the exact spot where airflow separates and lift collapses; the simulation is only as trustworthy as the mesh feeding it.
Flow Analysis — Pressure

Pressure difference is what generates lift.

Low pressure over the upper surface and high pressure beneath grow more pronounced as angle of attack increases from 0° to 13° — the pressure differential the wing rides on.

Why it matters
Why visualize pressure instead of just quoting a lift number?
It shows exactly where on the wing lift is being generated and how that shifts as angle of attack rises; the same picture reveals a stall coming before it happens.

06 — Result

A stable, high-payload airframe, validated end to end.
